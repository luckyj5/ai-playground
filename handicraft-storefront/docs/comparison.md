## Site-by-site notes

### 1. Jaypore — [jaypore.com](https://jaypore.com)

**The editorial aesthetic.** Jaypore built its brand on story-rich product
pages, curated "collections" around a craft cluster (e.g. Ajrakh, Kantha,
Chanderi) and a very tight visual language. Since being acquired by Aditya
Birla Fashion & Retail, it has moved slightly upmarket and added a stronger
private label.

- **Strengths:** quality curation, photography, storytelling, trust badges
  ("100% Authentic Products", "Quality Checked", "10-day free return"),
  global shipping.
- **Weaknesses:** catalog grows slowly because everything is editorial.
  Artisans can’t self-list. Discovery skews by category, not by craft
  tradition — a saree by "Chanderi weave" and "Ikat weave" look the same in
  the saree grid until you click.
- **What to copy:** trust-badge strip at the top of PDP; returns messaging;
  "story" module on product pages.

### 2. iTokri — [itokri.com](https://itokri.com)

**The long-tail champion.** iTokri crossed 100,000 live SKUs in April 2026
and adds 500+ products a day. Its USP is running fabrics by the metre and
very deep filters: Ajrakh, Bagru, Sanganeri, Kalamkari, Bandhani, Ikat and
150+ techniques, each with its own landing page.

- **Strengths:** catalog breadth, cluster-level pages, transparent pricing,
  ships to 8+ countries. 10,000+ artisan partners.
- **Weaknesses:** cataloguing is still manual — a 95%-women Gwalior ops
  team shoots, writes and uploads every listing. This is both the moat and
  the bottleneck. There is no public seller self-service.
- **What to copy:** filter-rich shop page; per-technique landing pages;
  "Crafts of India" navigation that doubles as education.

### 3. Okhai — [okhai.org](https://okhai.org)

**The mission-driven model.** Okhai began as a Tata Chemicals CSR initiative
in Gujarat and today works with 25,000+ women artisans. The brand leads
with empowerment — "Look Fabulous while Empowering Artisans" is the
on-site banner.

- **Strengths:** 30-day return window with pickup (unusually generous for
  handmade goods), clear refund policy ("Damaged / Defective / Not as
  Described" triggers no-questions replacement), strong artisan storytelling
  across the site.
- **Weaknesses:** narrow range outside of apparel and textiles; UX looks
  dated; limited payment options; no loyalty programme.
- **What to copy:** 30-day window worded around "damaged / defective / not
  as described"; prominent mission statement on the hero.

### 4. FabIndia — [fabindia.com](https://fabindia.com)

**The omnichannel incumbent.** Founded in 1960, 300+ physical stores, the
category leader for ethnic everyday. Selling well beyond crafts (organic
food, wellness), which is both strength and dilution.

- **Strengths:** best-in-class loyalty (FabFamily), EMI, gift cards, "book
  an appointment" for weddings, multi-country sites, super-strong returns
  logistics.
- **Weaknesses:** handicraft specificity has softened — most SKUs are
  designed in-house and manufactured in Fabindia-controlled units.
  Individual artisan attribution is weak on the PDP.
- **What to copy:** loyalty mechanics; gift cards; wedding appointments as
  a category; robust address book.

### 5. Amazon Karigar — [amazon.in/karigar](https://www.amazon.in/karigar)

**The marketplace funnel.** Amazon’s dedicated section for 12 lakh artisans
and 1 lakh+ handmade products. Uses Amazon’s full checkout, Prime logistics
where eligible, Amazon Pay UPI.

- **Strengths:** reach, trust, logistics, self-service seller onboarding via
  Seller Central, A-to-z guarantee.
- **Weaknesses:** handicrafts compete with mass-produced listings on the
  same result page, so discovery is noisy. Storytelling is weak — Amazon
  PDPs don’t reward "the 16-step Ajrakh process" copy.
- **What to copy:** self-service listing flow; A-to-z guarantee language;
  escrow-backed buyer protection.

## Where Kalakar Bazaar fits

Across the five, **none** offer a fast, self-service, image-led listing flow
for artisans + a curated buyer experience with good storytelling. The
closest combinations are:

- **Amazon Karigar** (self-service, low storytelling)
- **iTokri / Jaypore / Okhai** (great storytelling, no self-service — every
  listing is hand-made in Gwalior or Mumbai by a small ops team)

A modern, LLM-powered snap-and-list flow collapses that ops bottleneck
without losing the editorial quality — the photo is the input, the AI draft
is the first pass, the artisan is the editor-in-chief. That’s the moat this
demo is designed to explore.
